# Serendipity Protocol — L2 Methodology

## Purpose
This protocol defines how to search for Level 2 (serendipitous) evidence — clues the researcher did not explicitly ask for, found through adjacent language, cross-modal connections, and weak signals.

## When to use
- After completing L1 direct evidence retrieval
- Before writing a Mailbox note (Tacito mandatory pass)
- When the researcher asks a broad or open-ended question

## Methods

### Method 1: Metaphor mapping
Search for analogous expressions of the same underlying concept:
1. Identify the core concept (e.g., "speed vs. quality")
2. Generate semantic paraphrases: "fast but repair", "quick but clean", "first draft but rewrite"
3. Search indexes and fragments for these paraphrases
4. Record matches as L2 clues with the original concept noted

### Method 2: Cross-modal adjacency
Connect evidence across different modalities:
1. Start with a fragment from one modality (e.g., audio transcription)
2. Check if the SAME folder has other modalities (scans, handwritten notes, researcher annotations)
3. Look for the same topic expressed differently across modalities
4. A concept appearing in 2+ modalities is a stronger signal than in 1

### Method 3: Weak signal accumulation
Distinguish genuine weak signals from noise:
- 1 ambiguous fragment = noise, do not report
- 2 fragments from different folders = weak signal, report as L2 with low confidence
- 3+ fragments from 3+ folders = emerging pattern, report as L2 with medium confidence
- After back-search verification, may upgrade to L1

### Method 4: Negative space search
Look for what is NOT in the indexes:
1. Check `00_realm_index.md` for unmapped exercises
2. Check if any modality is entirely unindexed (e.g., "we have 20 audio files but 0 transcriptions")
3. Flag gaps in `05_agent_reports/` — absence of evidence is itself information

### Method 5: Marginalia and asides
Search for off-hand comments, metaphors, and figurative language:
1. In transcriptions, look for phrases that are NOT direct answers: jokes, hesitations, comparisons, hypotheticals
2. In handwritten notes, look for marginalia, sketches, underlinings, question marks
3. In researcher notes, look for "interesting" or "odd" or "notable" markers

## Reporting L2 findings

Every L2 entry must include:
- **Clue**: what was found
- **Method used**: which serendipity method produced it
- **Why it may matter**: connection to current research
- **Back-search needed?**: yes/no — if yes, what to check
- **Accumulation count**: how many related fragments exist across folders
- **Confidence**: low / medium / high

## Mandatory rules
1. No L2 clue may be reported without at least a partial back-search (source verification)
2. Every L2 clue must be labeled as L2 — do not upgrade to L1 without full verification
3. If an L2 clue survives back-search with high confidence, it may be upgraded to L1 but the upgrade must be noted
